Reviewer #1: This paper proposes MultiFusion, a hierarchical clustering post-processing method that introduces a new notion of "solidity" for internal nodes of a dendrogram. The method identifies "solid clusters" based on a formula combining node size and two dendrogram heights, optionally inserts "cork clusters" to cover remaining leaves, and selects among linkage methods by maximizing an aggregate solidity score. The authors claim that MultiFusion requires minimal parameter tuning, does not need the number of clusters a priori, and outperforms 11 popular clustering algorithms on 201 datasets using external validity indices. The following point need to be addressed:
1) The paper proposes solidity_i ~ N_i/h_i^2 * h′_i and also describes "density" as N_i divided by normalized distance. These relationships are never made precise. The use of "~" suggests proportionality but the actual constants, normalization, and invariances are unspecified. How do different scalings of the distance matrix (e.g., monotone transformations) affect h and h′, and thus solidity? Why should h′ be negative for the root, and what is the intended semantics?
2) The cluster extraction rule "children's solidity > parent's solidity" resembles a local salience/persistence test. This idea is reasonable, but without a formalization (e.g., consistency under small perturbations, guarantees on over- or under-segmentation), it remains heuristic. There is no proof of desirable properties like stability (cf. stability analyses for linkage methods) or robustness to single-linkage chaining effects.
3) Choosing a linkage by maximizing average solidity is an internal criterion, it could be useful, but the paper should show that higher aggregated solidity correlates with better external validity. Moreover, restricting to single and Ward by default may miss strong options (average, complete). recent theoretical results show nuanced trade-offs for these linkages.
4) The paper reports medians but no statistical tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank over per-dataset scores, Friedman/Nemenyi for multiple algorithms). Claims of "highest median" are insufficient without significance and effect sizes.
5) Authors should relate solidity to known notions such as HDBSCAN stability, Hartigan's excess-of-mass cluster tree, or persistence (prominence) in dendrograms. Are there conditions under which your rule is consistent or stable (e.g., under small perturbations)?

Reviewer #2:
The computational complexity and scalability of the proposed algorithm are not clearly analyzed or discussed.
The experimental comparison is potentially biased, as some baseline methods are given the true number of clusters while the proposed method is not.
